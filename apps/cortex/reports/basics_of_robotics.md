## Evaluation Metrics

Evaluating the performance of robotic systems necessitates quantifiable metrics to assess their effectiveness. Key metrics for object classification and identification include:

* **Precision:** Measures the proportion of correctly classified or identified objects among all classified or identified objects (Bishop, 2006).
* **Recall:** Measures the proportion of correctly classified or identified objects among all objects in the environment that should have been classified or identified.
* **Accuracy:** Represents the overall proportion of correctly classified or identified objects among all objects in the environment.
* **F1-score:** A harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a balanced measure of performance when both precision and recall are important (Bishop, 2006).

These interrelated measures offer valuable insights into a robot's ability to accurately classify or identify objects within its surroundings.

## 2.2 Control Systems in Robotics

Control systems are pivotal to the success of robotics, enabling robots to navigate their environment and perform tasks efficiently. This section explores the fundamental concepts and principles underpinning control systems in robotics.

**Open-Loop vs. Closed-Loop Control**

Open-loop control systems rely solely on programmed algorithms, devoid of feedback from the environment. Conversely, closed-loop control systems utilize sensors to monitor the surroundings and dynamically adjust robot actions accordingly. The prevalence of closed-loop control in robotics highlights its efficacy in adapting to dynamic conditions and optimizing performance.

**Feedback Mechanisms**

Central to closed-loop control are feedback mechanisms, facilitating continuous communication between the robot and its environment. This feedback can take various forms, including visual, auditory, or tactile information, empowering robots to respond appropriately to their surroundings.

**PID Controllers**

Proportional-Integral-Derivative (PID) controllers are widely employed in robotic systems. These controllers combine three components – proportional, integral, and derivative – to generate an output that corrects the discrepancy between the robot's desired state and its current state. Their simplicity and effectiveness have made PID controllers indispensable for numerous robotic applications.

**Applications of Control Systems in Robotics**

Control systems are ubiquitous in the realm of robotics, encompassing diverse robot types. For instance, industrial robots utilize closed-loop control to manipulate objects with precision. Similarly, autonomous vehicles leverage PID controllers to regulate speed and steering based on sensor feedback.

## 3. Types of Actuators

**3. Electric Actuators:**

* **Stepper motors:** Provide precise angular displacement but with limited torque.
* **Brushless DC motors:** Offer high torque and speed, making them suitable for various robotic applications.
* **Linear motors:** Generate linear motion, commonly used in robotic arms and mobile robots.

**4. Shape Memory Alloy (SMA) Actuators:**

SMA actuators utilize the shape memory effect of certain materials to generate force. They are lightweight and compact, making them ideal for miniature robots.

**5. Electrothermal Actuators:**

These actuators utilize temperature changes to expand or contract, generating force. They offer precise control and are suitable for applications where space is limited.

**6. Piezoelectric Actuators:**

These actuators utilize the piezoelectric effect to generate force or displacement when subjected to electrical signals. Their high precision and responsiveness make them suitable for micro-robotics.

## 1. PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) Control

PID control is a fundamental algorithm widely employed to control robotic movements across industries. To illustrate the working of PID control, imagine a robotic arm tasked with precisely tracking a target while maintaining a consistent speed. The PID controller continuously monitors the arm's movement, factoring in:

* **Proportional term:** Responds to current errors, instantly correcting deviations from the desired trajectory.
* **Integral term:** Compensates for accumulated errors over time, ensuring long-term accuracy.
* **Derivative term:** Anticipates future errors based on the rate of change, ensuring smooth and responsive adjustments.

By combining these elements, PID control empowers robots to achieve precise movement, maintain stability, and gracefully handle disturbances or uncertainties.
